FAQ


What exactly is a sloth?

Sloths are tree-dwelling mammals native to Central and South America. They belong to two main families — two-toed and three-toed sloths — named for the number of claws on their front limbs.

Why do sloths move so slowly?

Their slow movement is a survival strategy. It helps them conserve energy (since their diet is low-calorie leaves) and avoid predators by blending into the trees.

How long do sloths live?

In the wild, sloths live about 20–30 years. In captivity, with fewer predators and regular meals, some can live into their 40s.

Do sloths really sleep all day?

Not quite! Wild sloths sleep around 10 hours a day — less than the old myth of “20 hours.” The rest of the time they’re quietly munching leaves or hanging out (literally).

How often do sloths come down from the trees?

Only about once a week, usually to poop! The rest of their lives — eating, sleeping, even giving birth — happens in the trees.

What do sloths eat?

Mostly leaves, twigs, and fruit. They’re strict folivores (leaf-eaters), and their slow metabolism means they don’t need much food.

Are sloths good swimmers?

Surprisingly, yes! They can swim three times faster than they move on land, using a kind of doggy paddle and holding their breath for up to 40 minutes.

Do sloths have any predators?

Yes — jaguars, ocelots, harpy eagles, and large snakes like anacondas. Their best defense is staying motionless and camouflaged.

Why do sloths have green fur sometimes?

That green tint comes from algae growing on their fur. The algae help them blend in, and sloths even absorb nutrients from it through their skin.

Are sloths endangered?

Some species are. The Pygmy three-toed sloth, found only on a tiny island off Panama, is critically endangered. Habitat loss and deforestation are major threats.

Do sloths ever touch the ground?

Rarely. When they do, it’s awkward — they can’t walk well and have to drag themselves. They’re built for hanging, not hiking!

What’s the difference between two-toed and three-toed sloths?

  • Two-toed sloths: Slightly larger, nocturnal, and faster (relatively speaking).
  • Three-toed sloths: Smaller, diurnal (active by day), and have that iconic “smiling” face.
